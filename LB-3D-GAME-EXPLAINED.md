# Laura Biagiotti 3D game — what the client actually wants, explained from zero

For the build team. Assumes you know nothing about Italy, Rome, or perfume. Every Italian word, every place, and every product is explained the first time it appears.

This document is about **what** is being asked for. The stack recommendation is at the very end, in section 8, and deliberately kept short — because the plan we received was written by a non-technical creative, and the technical paragraphs in it are guesses that should not drive our decisions.

## 1. The brand, in plain words

**Laura Biagiotti** was an Italian fashion designer. The company is a fashion house; **Laura Biagiotti Parfums** is its perfume arm. The perfume business is run in partnership with **Angelini Beauty** — that's the client paying for this. **Lavinia Biagiotti** is the daughter, and appears on the current website, so she is a living person associated with the brand. **Laura Biagiotti herself is dead** (this matters: the brief is careful that the player character "is never named as her," and that instinct is correct).

The brand's signature colour is **white**. Its home city and its entire identity is **Rome**.

Their own website line, verified: _"A Made in Italy love story that has continued since 1988."_

The whole product line is named after Rome. This is not a coincidence the brief invented — it's the actual business.

> **The brief says there are three.** There are four. The brief's entire finale — "the three dry springs of the Aventine" — is built on a wrong number, and it omits the one marine fragrance. See section 7.

### Perfume vocabulary you'll need

- **Note** — a single smell ingredient. Bergamot is a note. Fig is a note.
- **Olfactory pyramid** — perfumes are described in three layers: **top notes** (what you smell in the first minutes, usually citrus, light), **heart notes** (the middle, usually flowers), **base notes** (what lingers for hours, usually wood, musk, vanilla). "Pyramid" because the layers stack.
- **Eau de Parfum / EDP** — a concentration strength. Stronger than Eau de Toilette. "Intense" is stronger again.
- **Vaporize** — the client's mandated word for "spray." Their tone-of-voice rules forbid "spray." _(Unverified — from the missing deck.)_
- **Flacon** — a small bottle. Just the fancy word for a perfume bottle.

## 2. The Rome references, explained

The game world is a tiny fantasy Rome. It is explicitly **not** historical or archaeological — it's "Rome as a feeling." Here is every place the brief names and what it actually is.

- **The Pantheon** — a real 2,000-year-old domed temple in central Rome, famous for a circular hole in the roof. The brief calls its game version "a Pantheon-like rotunda." _Rotunda_ just means a round building.
- **The Trevi Fountain** — Rome's most famous fountain, huge, baroque, crowded with tourists throwing coins. The game has a "Trevi-like fountain."
- **The Aventine** — one of Rome's seven hills, quiet and residential. It has a famous **orange garden** (_Giardino degli Aranci_), a public park planted with orange trees, with a view over the city. The game's finale happens there.
- **Trastevere** — a neighbourhood across the river. Narrow streets, old buildings, laundry hung between windows, considered the "authentic" working-class Rome. The game has "Trastevere rooftops with laundry lines."
- **The Tiber** (_Tevere_) — the river running through Rome. In the game it loops all the way around the mini-planet.
- **Alda Merini** — a real, famous Italian poet (1931–2009), known for writing about mental illness, love and self-worth. The brief says the client "loved" a thought of hers: that loving yourself is the source of happiness. Quest 2 paraphrases it. _(Unverified.)_
- **La Dolce Vita** — a 1960 Fellini film. Its most famous scene has a woman wading in the Trevi Fountain. It is _the_ visual cliché of glamorous Rome. Direction C riffs on it.
- **Vespa** — the Italian motor scooter. Another Rome/Italy visual cliché, from the film _Roman Holiday_.
- **#CineRoma** — an existing influencer campaign the client already runs. _Cine_ = cinema. _(Unverified.)_
- **Venezia** — Venice. Also, apparently, a **discontinued Laura Biagiotti perfume** that fans keep asking for. The easter egg is aimed at them. _(Unverified — I could not confirm a Venezia fragrance ever existed.)_

### Italian lines used in the game, translated

- **"Roma in un Respiro"** (the project title) — "Rome in One Breath." A breath, because perfume is inhaled.
- **"La Messaggera di Roma"** — "The Messenger Girl of Rome." _Messaggera_ is the feminine form of messenger.
- **"Il Giardino delle Essenze"** — "The Garden of Essences."
- **"Ciak, si profuma"** — a pun. _"Ciak, si gira"_ is what an Italian film director shouts to start filming (literally "Clapperboard, we're rolling"). Swapping _gira_ for _profuma_ makes it "Clapperboard, we're perfuming."
- **"E il tuo momento perfetto, che profumo ha?"** (the closing question) — "And your perfect moment — what does it smell like?"
- **"Fuori produzione, non fuori dal cuore. Un giorno, chissà."** (the easter egg) — "Out of production, not out of the heart. One day, who knows."

## 3. What the player actually does

Strip away the poetry. Mechanically, this is a **fetch-quest game**: five people want something, you go get it, you bring it back, you get a reward cutscene.

The whole thing lasts **6–9 minutes**. There is **no way to lose**. No timer, no enemies, no skill test, no death. If you wander off, nothing bad happens. This is a deliberate constraint because the buyers are 35–55-year-old women who have likely never played a video game.

### Minute by minute

**0:00 — The intro (scroll-driven).**
You land on a web page. You don't press play; you _scroll_. As you scroll, a small planet rotates in front of you. The buildings on its surface are arranged so that from this angle they spell out the word **ROMA**. Keep scrolling and a single yellow button appears.

The button says **VAPORIZE**, not "start" — because the button is shaped like the pump on top of a perfume bottle. You press it. The camera dives down through a burst of golden particles, as if you are falling into a cloud of perfume, and lands on the street.

**0:30 — You are the Messaggera.**
Third-person view: you see your character from behind, as in most console games. She is a young woman, early twenties, wearing white (the brand colour) with a soft cashmere scarf and a yellow bag slung across her body. The bag is full of small wrapped perfume bottles. She is a courier — a delivery person.

You move her with WASD/arrow keys on desktop, or a virtual thumbstick on a phone.

**0:30–8:00 — The loop.**
A checklist sits in a corner of the screen listing five deliveries. You walk to a person, a speech bubble conversation plays (3–4 exchanges), they ask for a small favour, you do the favour, you hand them the perfume, and a short cutscene plays. Repeat five times, in any order.

Walking the entire planet takes about 90 seconds, so nothing is ever far away.

**8:00 — The ending.**
The camera pulls back up to space. The planet is now at sunset, and every place you visited is visibly changed — lights are on, water is flowing, people are together. You are asked one question. You get a picture to share, a perfume recommendation, and an optional newsletter signup.

## 4. The five scenes, in full

Each scene follows the identical five-beat structure:

1. **Find** the person.
2. **Talk** — 3–4 speech-bubble exchanges.
3. **Task** — a small errand in the world.
4. **Deliver** the perfume.
5. **"The moment blooms"** — a ~5-second cutscene: the light changes, particles fly, one line of text appears. Then a **fragrance card** slides in: a picture of the bottle, its ingredient pyramid, a one-line story, and a link to buy it.

Each scene is built to sell exactly one product.

---

### Scene 1 — "The morning edition" → sells **ROMA UOMO** (men's)

**Where:** A newspaper kiosk at dawn, near the round Pantheon-like temple. (A _kiosk_ is a small street newsstand — a booth selling papers and cigarettes. Extremely common in Italian squares.)

**Who:** The kiosk owner, a man. His espresso has gone cold. In his words, his morning "has no shape."

**Task:** Cross the square to the bar, get a fresh coffee, bring it back — **without bumping into the pigeons.** (Not a fail state. Bumping them presumably just scatters them.)

> _A "bar" in Italy is a café. You stand at the counter, drink an espresso in 40 seconds, and leave. It is a morning ritual, not a place to drink alcohol. This is the single most Italian thing in the game and it will read as nonsense to a non-Italian unless the art makes it obvious._

**Delivery:** Roma Uomo.

**The moment blooms:** Morning light floods the square. Newspaper, steam rising off the coffee, "the city inhaling."

**Why this scene exists:** The brief says the client has _already proven_ that mornings + newspaper + coffee works in their social media posts. This is the safest scene in the game — it's reusing validated marketing territory. _(Unverified, but strategically the reason to open with it.)_

---

### Scene 2 — "Self-love, in verse" → sells **ROMA** (women's, the 1988 flagship)

**Where:** A rooftop in Trastevere, among the laundry lines.

**Who:** A **poetess** (a female poet). She is writing a poem addressed to herself and cannot finish the last line.

**Task:** The wind has blown three pages of her manuscript across the rooftops. Chase them down and bring them back.

**Delivery:** Roma.

**The moment blooms:** She finishes the poem. Her closing line paraphrases Alda Merini: _loving yourself is where happiness begins._

**Why this scene exists — read this carefully:** The brief states, explicitly, **"No male love interest; the moment is hers."** For a 1988 women's flagship perfume marketed to women aged 35–55, this is the single most important editorial decision in the whole game. It is not decoration. Do not let anyone "improve" it by adding a romance.

---

### Scene 3 — "White flowers, after dark" → sells **ROMA FIORI BIANCHI** (women's)

**Where:** A florist's shop and her terrace.

**Who:** A florist, closing up. She's hosting a dinner on her terrace tonight, and her **jasmine** hasn't opened yet. (Jasmine is a white flower.)

**Task:** Light the three lanterns around her terrace as dusk falls.

**Special mechanic:** **The world's lighting genuinely shifts from day to evening during this quest.** This is the only scene that changes the global time of day. It is a real technical requirement, not a metaphor — and it's the most expensive single feature in the game.

**Delivery:** Roma Fiori Bianchi ("Rome, White Flowers").

**The moment blooms:** The white flowers open, because many white flowers genuinely do open at night and release scent then. The product name, the mechanic, and a botanical fact all line up. This is the best-designed scene in the brief.

_The brief credits this to a 2026 "after-dark florals" trend sheet. (Unverified.)_

---

### Scene 4 — "The knot" → sells **FOREVER** (women's)

**Where:** The fountain square (the Trevi-like fountain).

**Who:** Two elderly people who always sit by the fountain. They've had an argument. He is sulking on a bench. She is feeding the cats and pretending not to look at him.

**Task:** Carry a note from one to the other, and back. **You choose between two possible replies** — both are kind, one is funnier. This is the only choice the player makes in the entire game, and it changes nothing but flavour.

**Delivery:** Forever — given to **both of them**, at the fountain.

**The moment blooms:** The fountain's water jets braid themselves into an **infinity symbol** (∞). The reason: the real Forever bottle has an **infinity knot** on it. The cutscene is a literal animation of the product's packaging.

---

### Scene 5 — "The three springs of the Aventine" → sells **AQVE ROMANE** (the finale)

**Where:** The orange garden on the Aventine hill.

**Who:** The gardener, who tends three marble **springs** (fountains where water emerges from the ground) that have run dry.

**Task:** This is where the collectible notes finally matter. Bring the right ingredients to the right spring:

| Spring | Ingredients to bring            | Perfume awakened   |
| ------ | ------------------------------- | ------------------ |
| 1      | Roman chamomile + medlar leaves | **Ambrosia Aurea** |
| 2      | Fig + artichoke + laurel        | **Divinum Ficus**  |
| 3      | Red grape + Aventine rose       | **Uva Dulcis**     |

_(Medlar is a small brown autumn fruit. Laurel is the bay leaf. Artichoke as a perfume note is unusual and specifically Roman — artichokes are a Roman culinary signature.)_

**If the player collected nothing, the game simply guides them through it.** No punishment.

**The moment blooms:** The three springs run **green, amber and ruby**. The unisex collection is presented as "the new chapter written by the next generation" — which is literally the young Messaggera you've been playing.

> **This scene is broken.** Aqve Romane has **four** fragrances. **Rubus Maris** — the marine one, "Bramble of the Sea" — is missing. See section 7.

---

### The five collectible ingredients mechanic

Scattered around the world are **12 glowing "notes"** — a bergamot fruit on a tree by the fountain, white flowers on a terrace, a fig by a garden wall, a grain of pink pepper at a market stall.

Walking through one adds it to a small **olfactory diary** you carry.

They are **completely optional** and gate nothing. But if you collect all the ingredients belonging to one perfume _before_ delivering it, you unlock **one extra secret line of dialogue** in that scene.

This is the cleverest idea in the brief. It teaches people what is actually inside each perfume without ever showing them an ingredients chart. It's product education disguised as picking up shiny things.

---

### The hidden scene — "A letter for Venezia"

At the bottom of your bag is a **sixth envelope** that appears on no checklist, addressed to Venezia (Venice).

Take it to the tiny harbour. The Messaggera admits no bridge reaches Venezia today. She puts the letter in a bottle and floats it out to sea, saying:

> _"Fuori produzione, non fuori dal cuore. Un giorno, chissà."_
> ("Out of production, not out of the heart. One day, who knows.")

This is a gift to the brand's fans, who apparently keep asking for the discontinued Venezia perfume to come back. _(Unverified.)_ **The brief flags that the client must approve this**, because it publicly acknowledges a dead product.

---

### The ending, in three parts

The camera rises. The planet sits at sunset, and every moment you created is now visibly alive down there: lights on the florist's terrace, the springs flowing, the old couple together at the fountain.

One question appears: **"E il tuo momento perfetto, che profumo ha?"** — _And your perfect moment, what does it smell like?_

Then:

1. **A share card.** An image of your planet with your completed moments and your collected ingredient pyramid, sized for an Instagram story.
2. **A fragrance recommendation.** Based on which scenes you lingered in and which ingredients you collected, the game suggests one perfume and links to its product page.
3. **A newsletter signup.** One line. The brief specifically says _"no modal aggression"_ — no pop-up that blocks the screen.

## 5. Every feature, listed

**Core**

- Scroll-driven intro where a planet rotates and buildings spell ROMA
- A VAPORIZE button shaped like a perfume pump
- Third-person character controller on a walkable spherical planet
- Free roam; ~90 seconds to walk the whole world
- Five quests, completable in any order
- Speech-bubble dialogue with NPCs
- A quest checklist UI
- Five "moment blooms" cutscenes
- Five fragrance cards with buy links

**Collectibles & customisation**

- 12 collectible olfactory notes, optional
- An olfactory diary UI
- Secret bonus dialogue for completing a pyramid before delivery
- Outfit changer: two alternate looks unlocked by progress

**World & delight**

- A day → dusk global lighting shift, triggered by scene 3
- Ambient NPCs
- **A cat on every quest route** (the brief's words: _"cats are free engagement"_)
- One hidden easter-egg scene
- Adaptive music that changes from day to dusk
- Sound effects, with a mute toggle

**Ending & business**

- Share-card image generation
- Fragrance-finder recommendation
- Newsletter opt-in
- Analytics: quest completion, time in world, card opens, outbound clicks, share generations
- UTM-tagged links per fragrance

**Platform**

- English and Italian at launch; German later
- Mobile-first touch controls, desktop keyboard
- Target: 60fps on a mid-range phone, interactive in under 4 seconds on 4G

## 6. The two alternatives, and why A wins

**Direction B — "The Garden of Essences."** One garden, no people, no deliveries. You wake the Aqve Romane perfumes by finding their ingredients. 3–4 minutes. Much cheaper: one environment, no dialogue system, no quests. It's an interactive poem. **Weakness: no human stories, so nothing for the community to quote back.**

**Direction C — "Ciak, si profuma."** Same courier mechanic, but the five deliveries happen on five film sets of an imaginary Roman movie shoot. **Strength: a perfect influencer hook** — celebrities can play it and post "their scene." **Risks:** the film imagery has to stay legally on the parody side of copyright, and it's a seasonal campaign rather than a permanent brand world.

**The recommendation is A**, and the argument is sound: A already contains B's ingredient mechanic inside scene 5, and C can later be added as "chapter 2" on the same planet and the same codebase. One build, three lives.

## 7. Problems the client needs to answer

**Three factual errors:**

1. **Aqve Romane has four fragrances, not three.** Rubus Maris — the marine one — is missing from the finale. Three dry springs cannot awaken four perfumes. Either the finale becomes **four springs**, or Rubus Maris gets its own scene. There is an elegant fix available: Rubus Maris means _"Bramble of the Sea"_ and is a marine fragrance, and the game **already has a harbour** (the Venezia easter egg happens there). A fourth, sea-facing spring at the harbour would solve the arithmetic and use an existing location.

2. **"Green Swing" is a men's fragrance, not an outfit.** The brief proposes unlocking "a Green Swing sporty variant" outfit for a female character. Roma Uomo Green Swing is a men's product. Either rename the outfit or accept that it markets a men's scent through a woman's wardrobe.

3. **The brief's technical paragraph about the reference game is wrong** on three counts. It doesn't matter for the client, but it means none of the brief's technical claims should be trusted without checking. Details are in `LB-3D-REFERENCE-TEARDOWN.md`.

**Missing inputs we cannot proceed without:**

- **The source decks.** The brand brief, the tone-of-voice document, the content strategy, the community management docs. Every rule about how the game talks comes from documents nobody on the build team has read.
- **The real olfactory pyramids.** The client's own website publishes only mood descriptions ("luminous, soft, unforgettable"), not top/heart/base ingredient lists. Scene 5's puzzle and the 12 collectibles both require exact ingredient lists per fragrance. **This is a hard blocker on the finale.**
- **3D references for the bottles**, or bottles suitable for photo-scanning. The Roma bottle is column-shaped; the Forever bottle has an infinity knot that scene 4 animates. These need to be accurate.
- **Approval for the Venezia easter egg**, which references a discontinued product.
- **Confirmation Venezia was ever a Laura Biagiotti fragrance.** I could not verify this.

**Decisions the client must make:**

- Which direction (A recommended).
- Where it lives: a subdomain of `laurabiagiottiparfums.com`, or a separate campaign domain.
- Whether "Laura Biagiotti" the person can be alluded to at all. She is deceased. The Messaggera is explicitly not her — but scene 5's "the next generation" line edges toward Lavinia Biagiotti, who is alive and public. Get this cleared.

## 8. Stack recommendation

Written only after reading the reference implementation. Full evidence in `LB-3D-REFERENCE-TEARDOWN.md`.

**Recommendation: Vite + React + React Three Fiber (with drei), three.js r180.**

_Not_ Next.js — this is a single-canvas app, not a content site; SSR buys nothing. _Not_ Svelte, even though the reference uses it, because our team ships React and the reference's code is closed anyway; there is nothing to copy line-by-line.

What we should copy from the reference, because it is proven:

| Concern          | Decision                                                              | Why                                                                                                   |
| ---------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Rendering        | three.js r180, toon material + gradient map                           | The 375-byte `atlas.png` holds the whole cel-shaded palette.                                          |
| Outlines         | Screen-space depth+normal edge pass, **not** inverted-hull            | Far cheaper; it's what the reference does.                                                            |
| Post-processing  | pmndrs `postprocessing` (bloom, godrays, DOF, SMAA, LUT grading)      | Same library the reference uses.                                                                      |
| Collision        | `three-mesh-bvh` **in a web worker**. No physics engine.              | The reference walks a whole planet with no Rapier/Cannon. Gravity is one constant.                    |
| Collision meshes | Author a simplified `hitmesh` per district                            | A real, unbudgeted art cost. Flag it now.                                                             |
| Geometry         | Draco-compressed, chunked terrain, 3–4 LODs                           | ~4 MB for the reference's terrain.                                                                    |
| Textures         | KTX2/Basis throughout                                                 | GPU-compressed.                                                                                       |
| Threading        | Push collision + character geometry off the main thread               | **This is how 60fps on mobile is actually achieved.** It is missing from the brief's production plan. |
| Quality tiering  | One `lowMemoryDevice` boolean swapping `low/` assets and mobile audio | Simple and sufficient.                                                                                |
| Embedding        | Expose a `start({container})` hook                                    | Keeps the "subdomain vs embedded in brand site" decision open.                                        |

Where we should deliberately **diverge** from the reference:

- **Dialogue as HTML/CSS overlay, not in-canvas text.** The reference renders text as MSDF glyphs inside the canvas. That is exactly why it is English-only and unreadable to a screen reader. Our overlay is what makes EN/IT/DE and accessibility possible. **This is a selling point — say so to the client.**
- **All copy in external JSON**, not compiled into the bundle as the reference does.
- **Ship an MP3/AAC audio fallback.** The reference is Ogg-only.
- **Ship a static hero + video fallback.** The reference renders a white page if WebGL fails.
- **Strip the debug GUI before release.** The reference shipped Tweakpane to production.
- **Analytics from day one.** The reference has _none_ — no GA4, no GTM, nothing. Our entire KPI list has no precedent to copy. It needs its own estimate.

Two things carry the most risk, and neither is a rendering problem:

1. **The share-card image generator** and the **fragrance-finder** are net-new. No reference exists.
2. **The day→dusk global lighting shift** in scene 3 is the most expensive single feature in the design, because it means every material, light and post-processing setting needs two states and a transition between them.
